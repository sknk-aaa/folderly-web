How to change a folder icon to an image on Windows 11

Open File Explorer and you're met with a wall of identical yellow folders. "Photos," "Invoices," "Last year's files" — different names, same look, and finding the right one takes a beat longer than it should. Sound familiar?

Here's the thing: on Windows you can give a folder any image you like as its icon. This guide covers the free built-in way first, then an easier way to use your own photos, and finally what to do when it just won't cooperate — step by step, no stumbling.

INTROCan you even change it?

Short answer: yes — and you don't need any special skills. Windows has a built-in option to change icons, and with a small extra app you can use your own photos directly as a folder's face.

Here's what it looks like in practice. On the left, before; on the right, with image icons. You can tell what's inside at a glance, so finding things takes far less time.

There are two main ways. Let's start with the one that costs nothing.

METHOD 1 ・ FREEChange a folder icon with Windows itself

No extra app required — Windows can do this on its own. The steps are simple.

  1. Right-click the folder you want to change and open Properties.
  2. Open the Customize tab and click Change Icon near the bottom.
  3. Pick one from the list, or choose your own .ico file, and click OK. Done.

The catch with the built-in method

It looks easy, but if you want to use your own photo, a few things get in the way. Honestly:

  • Only the .ico format works. Photos from your phone or camera (JPG / PNG) can't be selected as-is.
  • To use a photo, you first have to find and run a converter to make an .ico.
  • If you later move or delete that .ico, the icon reverts (it only remembers the location).
  • You can't fine-tune the position or size.

What begins as "let me just try it" often stalls right around the converter step — it's a common story.

What if you could just drag a photo in and be done?

TROUBLESHOOTINGIf it won't change or reverts

"I set it but nothing shows up." "It quietly went back to yellow." Here are the quick fixes.

  • The icon reverts: the built-in .ico method breaks if you move or delete the file. Keep it in place, or use a method that manages the file internally, like Folderly.
  • The change doesn't show / is slow: Windows caches icons. Reopen the folder or Explorer window to refresh (Folderly refreshes automatically after applying).
  • You can't pick a photo: the built-in tool only accepts .ico. To use a photo as-is, use Folderly.
Good to know: applied icons are stored in the folder via a file called desktop.ini. Folderly records the original state, so you can always restore the folder if you change your mind.
